\subsection{Specific handling editor comments}

\commentheading{\HE{1}}
\begin{commentblock}
- The abstract purports a fast approach to the proposed DQLM framework, but that doesn't seem to be verified anywhere in the manuscript -- what is the computational time and complexity of the proposed method? Once it is fitted, does it need to be reestimated in future months or years? Is it feasible to do so?
\end{commentblock}

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree, and the revised manuscript now states the computational cost and operational feasibility explicitly rather than leaving them implicit in the abstract.

To answer the editor's three questions directly: on runtime, under our production implementation, one full issuance cycle for the main model at a single river site requires about two hours end-to-end. That corresponds to roughly 100 minutes for fitting and about 20 additional minutes for post-processing on our Linux production server (64 CPU cores and about 503 GiB RAM). The seven quantile fits are run in parallel, so wall-clock time is governed by the slowest quantile fit rather than by the sum across all seven fits. On re-estimation, yes, the model is intended to be refit as new forecast issuances arrive. On feasibility, yes, that daily refitting is operationally feasible once the data pipeline is in place.

The dominant computational cost is repeated Kalman filtering/smoothing within iterative inference. This is not specific to our formulation: for state-space models fit by iterative procedures, the standard computational bottleneck is the repeated forward-backward updating of the latent state trajectory. Let \(T\) denote the historical length, \(H\) the forecast horizon, and \(d\) the latent-state dimension (\(d=31\) in our implementation). With fixed observation dimension, the leading cost of one full filtering/smoothing pass is
\[
O\!\left((T+H)\,d^{3}\right),
\]
with lower-order terms of order \(O((T+H)\,d^{2})\).

This also explains why we describe the VB implementation as the practically faster route for forecasting. For full MCMC, the total cost scales as
\[
O\!\left(I_{\mathrm{MCMC}}\,(T+H)\,d^{3}\right),
\]
where \(I_{\mathrm{MCMC}}\) includes burn-in, retained iterations, and any additional iterations required for adequate mixing. In other words, each MCMC iteration pays for another full filtering/smoothing pass. For VB-CAVI, the total cost scales as
\[
O\!\left(I_{\mathrm{VB}}\,(T+H)\,d^{3}\right),
\]
where \(I_{\mathrm{VB}}\) is the number of iterations required for ELBO convergence. For the forecasting objective of this paper, the practical distinction is that once VB has converged, posterior samples can be generated at low incremental cost, whereas MCMC continues to pay the full state-smoothing cost at every retained iteration.

This interpretation is also consistent with our simulation evidence. Although that simulation study was conducted in the simpler univariate DQLM/exDQLM setting implemented in the CRAN R package \texttt{exdqlm}, it still shows a substantial VB speed advantage, with VB approximately 10x--20x faster than MCMC in comparable fits. Because the application in this paper is multivariate and therefore more demanding, we treat that result as supporting evidence about relative algorithmic efficiency rather than as a one-to-one runtime claim for the present forecasting application.

To answer the re-estimation question more concretely, at issuance date 2022-12-25 we use all information available through 2022-12-25 (historical segment: 1987-05-29 to 2022-12-25) and produce corrected predictive distributions for 2022-12-26 through 2023-01-22. At the next issuance date, the model is refit because the information set has changed: the observed discharge record is extended, the retrospective products are updated, and new ensemble forecasts and forecast covariates become available.

In addition, posterior summaries from the previous issuance provide informed initial values for the next VB run, which can further reduce convergence time. Even without these warm starts, the observed end-to-end timing remains operationally feasible for daily forecasting at a single river site when the data pipeline is already in place.
\end{responsebox}

\begin{commentblock}
- One of the main issues with the paper is that the proposed method is not validated properly -- there are no competing alternatives (despite models, A, B and C being listed), the extended asymmetric Laplace is not really given validation either (how does this compare against just an asymmetric Laplace in this problem setting?). \responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree, and the revised manuscript now validates the proposed method against both simpler Bayesian competitors and raw physical forecast ensembles. In the revised comparison, the best version of the proposed model, \texttt{exAL-M-T1}, achieves the lowest forecast-window CRPS in four of the five cutoffs and remains below the corresponding \texttt{AL-M-T1} score in all five. This directly addresses the two main concerns raised here: whether the extended asymmetric Laplace formulation improves on the ordinary asymmetric Laplace in this forecasting problem, and whether the added model complexity yields practical gains relative to simpler statistical and physical baselines.

In the revised manuscript, we compare nine Bayesian alternatives built on the same linear-dynamic backbone (trend, seasonal, and transfer-function components) and Gaussian state-evolution noise. They differ only in two respects: the observation likelihood, and whether external forecast information is used during the forecast window.

We use the compact label \(L\)-\(S\)-\(T\). Here \(L\in\{\mathrm{N},\mathrm{AL},\mathrm{exAL}\}\), where N denotes Normal Dynamic Linear Model (NDLM), AL denotes Dynamic Quantile Linear Model (DQLM) with asymmetric Laplace likelihood, and exAL denotes exDQLM with extended asymmetric Laplace likelihood. Next, \(S\in\{\mathrm{U},\mathrm{M}\}\), where U is univariate (no retrospective products or forecast ensembles from climate institutions) and M is multivariate (uses retrospective products and forecast ensembles from ECMWF/GloFAS and NOAA/NWM). Finally, \(T\in\{\mathrm{T0},\mathrm{T1}\}\), where T0 means no transfer function during the forecast window and T1 means the transfer function remains active during the forecast window, using forecast precipitation and soil-moisture covariates from NOAA/GEFS.

This comparison addresses the extended asymmetric Laplace question directly. Under the strongest multivariate transfer-function specification, \texttt{exAL-M-T1} has lower forecast-window CRPS than \texttt{AL-M-T1} at all five cutoffs. More broadly, \texttt{exAL-M-T1} is the best-performing model in four of the five cutoffs, while the corrected \texttt{N-M-T1} row is lowest at 12/25/2022. The additional exAL complexity is therefore not only theoretical; it delivers the strongest operational performance in most cutoffs and remains highly competitive even in the final winter fold.

The broader validation message is straightforward. First, the multivariate transfer-enabled asymmetric-Laplace synthesis models (\texttt{AL-M-T1} and \texttt{exAL-M-T1}) provide the strongest and most consistent CRPS performance overall. Second, after rerunning the three NDLM families and the two univariate AL/exAL families under the corrected shared featurecov/blended-input contract, the Normal-likelihood alternatives remain less competitive in most cutoffs, though the corrected \texttt{N-M-T1} specification is strongest at 12/25/2022. Rather than asserting superior forecast quality by inspection alone, we now support the forecasting claim with explicit probabilistic scores: HE-2 reports CRPS across all model families and raw ensembles, and HE-4 reports targeted quantile check losses for the key synthesis competitors. \begin{responsebox}
We agree, and the revised manuscript now includes a targeted ablation study of the strongest model, \texttt{exAL-M-T1}, to identify which structural components contribute most to forecast skill.

Following the main validation in HE-2, we take the cutoff-specific winning \texttt{exAL-M-T1} configuration as the reference and remove one component at a time, while keeping the cutoffs, preprocessing, hyperparameters, external inputs, and evaluation protocol fixed. This design makes the comparison directly interpretable: any change in forecast-window CRPS can be attributed to the removed component rather than to retuning or to a different data configuration. We also verified that each ablation is a strict simplification of the corresponding full model: it uses the same covariate bundle, the same lagged and interaction terms in the transfer-function inputs, the same fixed discount factors and other fit hyperparameters, and changes only the intended structural component.

We use the following labels in the ablation study:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{exAL-M-T1} (full reference): trend + all three seasonal harmonic pairs + transfer-function components; transfer function active in both historical fitting and forecast window;
\item \texttt{exAL-M-T1-noTrend}: same as full model, but with trend component removed;
\item \texttt{exAL-M-noTF}: same as the full model, but with transfer information removed from both historical fitting and the forecast window. This is not the same comparison as the broader T0 family in HE-2, because here the rest of the winning \texttt{exAL-M-T1} specification is held fixed.
\item \texttt{exAL-M-T1-noH1}, \texttt{exAL-M-T1-noH2}, and \texttt{exAL-M-T1-noH3}: same as the full model, but with the first, second, or third retained seasonal harmonic pair removed from the seasonal state block. In the full model, the seasonal block retains three harmonic pairs (\texttt{harmonics = 1, 2, 1/6.8333333}), and each ablation removes exactly one of those three pairs. This design is more informative than removing the entire seasonal block at once because it shows whether forecast skill depends on a specific retained harmonic pair or on the seasonal block more broadly.
\end{itemize}

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree, and we have expanded the forecasting validation accordingly.

First, CRPS is now reported explicitly for the forecasting exercise, not only for estimation. In particular, HE-2 reports CRPS across cutoffs, benchmark models, and raw forecast products, so the revised manuscript now includes a proper probabilistic forecast-verification score for the out-of-sample forecasting exercise.

Second, we clarify why CRPS is the primary summary score for the synthesized posterior predictive distributions. For predictive CDF \(F\), observation \(y\), and quantile function \(q_\tau=F^{-1}(\tau)\),
\[
\mathrm{CRPS}(F,y)=2\int_0^1 \rho_\tau(y-q_\tau)\,d\tau,\qquad
\rho_\tau(u)=u\left(\tau-\mathbf{1}\{u<0\}\right),
\]
so CRPS is an integrated quantile-score criterion. This makes CRPS a natural primary metric for our forecasting problem, because the final object of interest is the synthesized posterior predictive distribution rather than any single quantile in isolation.

In plain terms, CRPS summarizes the quality of the full predictive distribution, whereas quantile check loss tells us whether the forecast is accurate at a specific targeted quantile level.

Third, to provide the explicit quantile-level diagnostics requested by the editor, we additionally report quantile check losses at the targeted levels \(\tau\in\{0.05,0.20,0.35,0.50,0.65,0.80,0.95\}\), using USGS observations as verification targets. We include these diagnostics for the four key models \texttt{exAL-M-T1}, \texttt{AL-M-T1}, \texttt{exAL-U-T1}, and \texttt{AL-U-T1}. This keeps the comparison focused on the main synthesis competitors while still showing how forecast quality changes across the targeted quantiles.

For \texttt{exAL-M-T1} and \texttt{AL-M-T1}, we use the corresponding final multivariate forecasting runs reported in HE-2; for \texttt{exAL-U-T1} and \texttt{AL-U-T1}, we use the carried-forward univariate baseline runs retained for the quantile-loss comparison, rather than the later corrected univariate HE-2 mean-CRPS rerun.

\begin{responsebox}
We agree that fair assessment of this method must be organized around forecast origins, not around a conventional single split of the time series.

The main reason is that our setting is not a standard univariate forecasting problem in which one fits a model to past USGS observations and then extrapolates that same series forward recursively. At a cutoff date \(\textbf{c}\), the method uses all information available through \(\textbf{c}\): observed discharge up to \(\textbf{c}\), retrospective or historical products available at \(\textbf{c}\), and the forecast ensembles and forecast covariates issued at that cutoff. Using the discrepancy structure learned before \(\textbf{c}\), it then produces predictive quantile estimates for the unobserved future USGS discharge over the forecast window covered by the forecast ensembles. These predictive quantile estimates are subsequently utilized to produce a synthesized predictive distribution. The predictive performance of the synthesized distribution is carried out only against the USGS observations realized after \(\textbf{c}\). For this reason, the appropriate evaluation framework is a rolling-origin backtesting.

This is already the logic of the revised five-cutoff analysis. The five cutoffs act as five temporally separated rolling-origin evaluation folds, each defined by valid archive availability and version consistency of the required inputs. In that sense, the revised analysis is already a fair out-of-sample forecasting assessment for this framework. It is also important that such cutoffs not be taken too densely in time: if forecast origins are too close to one another, their forecast windows may overlap heavily and repeatedly sample the same hydrological regime, which can overrepresent a particular setting in the aggregate validation.

More generally, the same design extends naturally to broader validation or hyperparameter tuning. For a given candidate setting, one may repeat the full cutoff-specific workflow over a larger set of feasible cutoff dates: fit the quantile models at each cutoff, obtain the forecast-window predictive quantile outputs, synthesize them into a predictive distribution, compute a proper score such as mean forecast-window CRPS, and then average that score across cutoffs. In practice, this larger cutoff set should still be chosen carefully so that the folds remain informative and do not collapse into many near-duplicate forecast situations. Repeating this procedure across candidate settings yields the specification with the best average predictive performance over the rolling-origin folds. This is the appropriate analogue of cross-validation in our setting.

The main limitation is practical rather than conceptual. Each cutoff requires a complete, version-consistent input bundle that includes retrospective products, forecast ensembles, and historical forecast covariates such as precipitation and soil moisture. The feasible cutoff range is therefore constrained by archive coverage and version matching across products, and a large rolling-origin study over all feasible cutoffs is computationally expensive even though it is parallelizable in principle. We will make this evaluation setup more explicit in the revised manuscript so that the role of the five-cutoff study and how it can be extended to broader tuning or validation exercises are clear.
\end{responsebox}
