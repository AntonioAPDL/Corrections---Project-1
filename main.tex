\subsection{Specific handling editor comments}

\commentheading{\HE{1}}
\begin{commentblock}
- The abstract purports a fast approach to the proposed DQLM framework, but that doesn't seem to be verified anywhere in the manuscript -- what is the computational time and complexity of the proposed method? Once it is fitted, does it need to be reestimated in future months or years? Is it feasible to do so?
\end{commentblock}

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree that computational feasibility must be stated explicitly, and we revised the manuscript accordingly.

For our main model, let \(T\) be the historical length, \(H\) the forecast length, and \(d\) the latent-state dimension (here \(d=31\)). The dominant cost in iterative inference is repeated Kalman filtering/smoothing, with leading order
\[
O\!\left((T+H)\,d^{3}\right),
\]
plus lower-order observation-assimilation terms \(O((T+H)\,d^{2})\).

Hence, for MCMC (burn-in \(B\), retained draws \(S\), thinning interval \(L\), and additional iterations for mixing \(M_{\text{mix}}\)):
\[
\mathcal{C}_{\text{MCMC}}
=
O\!\left((B+LS+M_{\text{mix}})\,(T+H)\,d^{3}\right).
\]
For VB-CAVI (with \(I_{\text{VB}}\) iterations to ELBO convergence):
\[
\mathcal{C}_{\text{VB}}
=
O\!\left(I_{\text{VB}}\,(T+H)\,d^{3}\right).
\]
This is the core reason we do not use full MCMC in the main operational analysis: MCMC must pay burn-in, retained-chain sampling, and often additional iterations when tail-quantile chains mix slowly, while VB requires convergence once and then allows posterior sampling at low incremental cost.

In our production runs for this work, the seven quantile fits are launched in parallel (one per quantile). Therefore, wall time is controlled by the slowest quantile fit (typically an extreme tail quantile), not by the sum across quantiles. Empirically, in the most demanding cutoff, the slowest quantile converged in about \(31.82\)--\(31.94\) minutes. The synthesis step is then applied over a short forecast window (\(H=28\) days) and is also parallelizable. From start to finish, model fit plus synthesis is operationally around 35 minutes via VB.

\textbf{TODO: This choice is also supported by our simulation study: preliminary univariate DQLM/exDQLM comparisons indicate VB is approximately 15x--24x faster than MCMC (reference and full details to be added in the revised manuscript).}

Regarding re-estimation: yes, the model should be re-estimated as new forecast issuances arrive. From a practitioner viewpoint, this is expected and feasible at daily resolution. Example: at issuance date 2022-12-25, we use data up to 2022-12-25 (historical segment: 1987-05-29 to 2022-12-25) and produce corrected predictive distributions from 2022-12-26 to 2023-01-22. At issuance date 2022-12-26, the model is refit because inputs are updated: one new observation, one new retrospective value from each provider (NOAA/NWM and ECMWF/GloFAS), and a newly issued ensemble forecast set from each provider.

A typical daily operational cycle is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item ingest retrospectives up to the current cutoff date (or append only the new day if historical data are already stored);
\item ingest forecast ensembles from the same issue date (here, GloFAS horizon \(=28\) days and NWM horizon \(\approx 10\) days);
\item ingest deterministic covariate forecasts up to the maximum synthesis horizon (for example, GEFS at 30 days, which exceeds the 28-day maximum ensemble horizon used here);
\item fit the seven quantile models and synthesize the corrected predictive distribution (about 35 minutes for our model in the demanding case).
\end{enumerate}

With an established data pipeline, this full cycle is operationally feasible for daily updates.
\end{responsebox}

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree with this concern, and we have expanded the validation panel accordingly.

In the revised manuscript, we now compare both physical and statistical references using a unified modeling backbone. All nine Bayesian alternatives use the same linear dynamic specification (trend, seasonal, and transfer-function components) and Gaussian state-evolution noise; the differences are in the observation likelihood and in how external forecast information is integrated during the forecast window. We use the compact label \(L\)-\(S\)-\(T\): \(L\in\{\mathrm{N},\mathrm{AL},\mathrm{exAL}\}\), where N denotes Normal Dynamic Linear Model (NDLM), AL denotes Dynamic Quantile Linear Model (DQLM) with asymmetric Laplace likelihood, and exAL denotes extended DQLM (exDQLM) with extended asymmetric Laplace likelihood; \(S\in\{\mathrm{U},\mathrm{M}\}\), where U is univariate (no retrospective products or forecast ensembles from climate institutions) and M is multivariate (uses retrospective products and forecast ensembles from ECMWF/GloFAS and NOAA/NWM); \(T\in\{\mathrm{T0},\mathrm{T1}\}\), where T0 means no transfer function during the forecast window, and T1 means transfer function active during the forecast window (requiring forecast precipitation and soil-moisture covariates, here from NOAA/GEFS).

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree, and we have expanded the forecasting validation accordingly.

First, CRPS is now reported explicitly for the forecasting exercise (not only for estimation), across all cutoffs and benchmark models (see HE-2 tables). This directly addresses the request for proper probabilistic forecast verification metrics.

Second, we clarify why CRPS is our primary score for synthesized posterior predictive distributions. For predictive CDF \(F\), observation \(y\), and quantile function \(q_\tau=F^{-1}(\tau)\),
\[
\mathrm{CRPS}(F,y)=2\int_0^1 \rho_\tau(y-q_\tau)\,d\tau,\qquad
\rho_\tau(u)=u\left(\tau-\mathbf{1}\{u<0\}\right),
\]
so CRPS is an integrated quantile-score criterion. In practice, with quantile levels \(\{\tau_k\}_{k=1}^K\), we use the standard discrete approximation
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{CRPS}}(F,y)\approx \frac{2}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \rho_{\tau_k}(y-q_{\tau_k}).
\]

Third, to provide explicit quantile-level diagnostics as requested, we additionally report quantile check losses at the targeted levels \(\tau\in\{0.05,0.20,0.35,0.50,0.65,0.80,0.95\}\), using USGS observations as verification targets. We include these diagnostics for the four key models:
\texttt{exAL-M-T1}, \texttt{AL-M-T1}, \texttt{exAL-U-T1}, and \texttt{AL-U-T1}.

Reviewer 1 Comments to the Author

This paper develops a Bayesian quantile regression approach, based on asymmetric Laplace distributions, to predict and forecast streamflow in the Santa Cruz region of the US. The work appears strong in the development of the Bayesian models and the novelty in the extension of inference methods is accepted. The paper is framed as a forecasting paper, however the forecasting component is limited and robust forecast evaluation is absent. I recommend that some substantial revisions, mainly structural, are required before proceeding with publication. My comments below don’t cover everything, but are intended as a push into reshaping:

\subsection{Reviewer 1 --- Major comments }
\begin{commentblock}
Major comments:
\end{commentblock}

\begin{commentresponse}[7]{\RoneM{1}}
1. In the introduction and elsewhere, the “cascade of uncertainty” is not distinguished between meteorological uncertainty and hydrological uncertainty. This is not necessarily a major problem with a Bayesian model that combines sources of uncertainty, but I find the introduction and motivation less compelling when hydrological and meteorological concepts are mixed. For example, the paragraph that starts off “Hydrological predictions are often produced using physical models…” diverts to discussions of under-dispersion in ensembles and perturbation methods in weather forecasts. I would suggest restructuring the introduction to cover different sources of uncertainty before explaining how uncertainty can be consolidated in a Bayesian framework.
\end{commentresponse}

\begin{commentresponse}[7]{\RoneM{2}}
2. The links between the model formulation and the results are not clear to me. For example, three models (A, B and C) are developed in section 2. It would be helpful to explicitly link the models to the results being discussed in section 3.
\end{commentresponse}

\begin{commentresponse}[7]{\RoneM{3}}
3. The paper is maths heavy, so it may be beneficial to reduce the equations and mathematical details. The amount of detail given may be highly suitable for a dissertation, but some details are not necessary for a paper. For example, in section 2.7, PITs are described in detail, however, it is followed that CRPS is chosen for model selection instead and PITs are not used. Suggest removing PITs in this case. As another example, no quantile crossing is observed, however a two-step method is presented to “resolve” quantile crossing. I suggest reconsidering how much detail is needed for the Posterior Predictive Synthesis part.
\end{commentresponse}

\begin{commentresponse}[7]{\RoneM{4}}
4. If I have not misunderstood, only one short forecast has been evaluated for a moderate flood from 2022-12-25 to 2023-01-22. Is it possible to provide more evidence for the use of the model for forecasting high, low and moderate events? Or the continuous period 2023 to present? Going to the previous comment, PITs and CRPS have been used for model selection (section 2.7) rather than forecast verification. I would suggest that in an expanded evaluation of forecast performance that CRPS and PITs would be useful tools.
\end{commentresponse}

\begin{commentresponse}[7]{\RoneM{5}}
5. A robust discussion of how the method can be set up for fair forecast assessment using cross-validation might be required.
\end{commentresponse}
