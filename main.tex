Dear Professor Fass\`o,

We thank you and the reviewer for the careful reading of our manuscript and for the constructive suggestions. We found the comments very helpful and have revised the paper substantially in response.

The revision described below strengthens the forecasting evaluation and clarifies the manuscript structure. In particular, we rebuilt the forecasting analysis around five version-consistent rolling-origin cutoffs, added direct comparisons against simpler Bayesian alternatives and the raw ECMWF/GloFAS and NWS forecast products, added forecast-window CRPS and targeted quantile diagnostics, and included a targeted ablation study of the selected extended-likelihood multivariate specification. We also revised the manuscript to clarify the roles of the different data sources, streamline parts of the methodological exposition, and make the computational and reproducibility discussion more explicit.

During the revision we also re-audited the retrospective and forecast-product archives used to construct the cutoff-specific input bundles. This audit identified a version-matching inconsistency in the historical support used by the original analysis. We rebuilt the affected rolling-origin bundles under a version-consistent contract, reran the affected model comparisons, and regenerated the manuscript-facing tables and figures from those corrected bundles.

Detailed point-by-point responses follow below.

\section*{Summary of Major Revisions}
\begin{itemize}
\item[] Rebuilt the forecasting validation around five version-consistent rolling-origin cutoffs and compared the proposed method against simpler Bayesian alternatives and the raw ECMWF/GloFAS and NWS forecast products.
\item[] Added forecast-window CRPS and targeted quantile diagnostics, and aligned the final forecasting protocol with the most recent forecast issuance available at each cutoff.
\item[] Added a targeted ablation study of the selected extended-likelihood multivariate specification and clarified the forecasting contribution of the Bayesian quantile-based correction-and-synthesis framework.
\item[] The manuscript revisions clarify the roles of observations, retrospective products, and forecast products, tighten the methodological exposition, and expand the computational and reproducibility discussion.
\end{itemize}

\section{Response to the Handling Editor}

\begin{commentblock}
Handling editor comment:

This paper develops a Bayesian dynamical model approach for river flow forecasting. A reviewer and I have read the manuscript, and although this is an important problem, and the proposed methods are interesting, there are some serious shortcomings of the submitted manuscript that need to be addressed.
\end{commentblock}


\commentheading{\HE{1}}
\begin{commentblock}
- The abstract purports a fast approach to the proposed DQLM framework, but that doesn't seem to be verified anywhere in the manuscript -- what is the computational time and complexity of the proposed method? Once it is fitted, does it need to be reestimated in future months or years? Is it feasible to do so?
\end{commentblock}

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
Thank you for pointing this out. The revised manuscript now makes the computational cost and operational feasibility of the forecasting workflow explicit in the posterior-computation discussion. For the main single-site workflow, the latest shared validation runtime records a total issuance-cycle benchmark of about two hours end-to-end on our production Linux server with 64 cores and roughly 503 GiB RAM, with the seven quantile-specific fits dispatched in parallel. We report this as a total wall-clock benchmark because the reliable populated timing fields in the shared interface are \texttt{runtime\_sec\_total} and \texttt{runtime\_sec}; the available metadata do not support a separate fitting/forecasting decomposition. The same runtime audit records 1620 completed measured interface rows, while the companion planned-run manifest preserves 54 completed and 18 pending planned run units, so we do not claim that every planned orchestration row completed. The model requires refitting as new observations, retrospective products, and forecast inputs become available, and the measured total runtime indicates that daily refitting is operationally feasible at this single-site scale once the data pipeline is in place. The dominant computational bottleneck is repeated Kalman filtering and smoothing, with leading-order cost proportional to \((T+H)d^{3}\) per full pass, where \(T\) is the historical length, \(H\) the forecast horizon, and \(d\) the latent-state dimension.
\end{responsebox}

%
\commentheading{\HE{2}}
\begin{commentblock}
- One of the main issues with the paper is that the proposed method is not validated properly -- there are no competing alternatives (despite models, A, B and C being listed), the extended asymmetric Laplace is not really given validation either (how does this compare against just an asymmetric Laplace in this problem setting?). I imagine this hierarchical model is likely so complicated that no working hydrologists would understand, nor use it, without very clear evidence that it was substantially superior to other forecast products. \responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree that the original manuscript did not validate the proposed forecasting framework strongly enough. In the revised manuscript, we address that directly through a five-cutoff rolling-origin out-of-sample evaluation. At each cutoff, we generate predictive distributions over the 28-day GloFAS/Bayesian forecast window and verify them against future USGS observations that were not used in model fitting. Because the archived NWS forecasts provide eight valid daily leads for these origins, we report NWS in a separate horizon-matched eight-day comparison.

Constructing this validation required recovering and harmonizing historical deterministic forecast archives, retrospective products, and forecast covariates across institutions. In the course of this revision, we also corrected the earlier retrospective and forecast-product version-matching issue so that the final comparisons use only version-consistent forecasting folds.

To describe the models considered, we use the compact label \(L\)-\(S\)-\(T\). Here \(L\in\{\mathrm{N},\mathrm{AL},\mathrm{exAL}\}\), where N denotes Normal Dynamic Linear Model (NDLM), AL denotes Dynamic Quantile Linear Model (DQLM) with asymmetric Laplace likelihood, and exAL denotes exDQLM with extended asymmetric Laplace likelihood. The \(N\) rows are Gaussian DLM baselines scored through posterior predictive distributions, not fitted quantile-lane models. Next, \(S\in\{\mathrm{U},\mathrm{M}\}\), where U is univariate (no retrospective products or forecast ensembles from climate institutions) and M is multivariate (uses retrospective products and forecast ensembles from ECMWF/GloFAS and NWS). Finally, \(T\in\{\mathrm{T0},\mathrm{T1}\}\), where T0 means no transfer function during the forecast window and T1 means the transfer function remains active during the forecast window, using forecast precipitation and soil-moisture covariates from NOAA/GEFS.

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree. In the revised manuscript, forecast-window CRPS is now reported explicitly for the out-of-sample forecasting exercise, and we also add targeted quantile check-loss diagnostics. The CRPS comparison across cutoffs, benchmark models, and raw forecast products is reported in HE-2.

We use CRPS as the primary summary score because the object of interest is the full synthesized predictive distribution rather than any single quantile in isolation, and CRPS is an integrated quantile-score criterion. Following the proper-scoring-rule perspective of Gneiting and Raftery (2007), we complement that full-distribution summary with quantile check losses at the targeted levels \(\tau\in\{0.05,0.20,0.35,0.50,0.65,0.80,0.95\}\), using USGS observations as verification targets. We include these diagnostics for the four main synthesis competitors \texttt{exAL-M-T1}, \texttt{AL-M-T1}, \texttt{exAL-U-T1}, and \texttt{AL-U-T1}, so the reader can see how quantile-level performance changes across cutoff windows.

\commentheading{\HE{6}}
\begin{commentblock}
- Something that wasn't clear to me in the application was: is this in-sample, or out-of-sample validation? It appears that the model was fit to the data that is being used in validation, which isn't realistic. Please perform an appropriate cross-validation study to investigate the approach's actual ability to forecast unseen data.
\end{commentblock}

\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree that this needed to be clarified. The revised forecasting evaluation is out-of-sample and uses five rolling-origin cutoff-based forecasting folds.

For each cutoff date \(\textbf{c}\), the model is fit using only observations and retrospective information available through \(\textbf{c}\): observed discharge up to \(\textbf{c}\) and retrospective/historical products available at \(\textbf{c}\). Forecasts are then generated over the post-cutoff forecast window using the latest forecast products issued at or before \(\textbf{c}\), together with the forecast-window precipitation and soil-moisture covariates staged in the same version-consistent origin bundle. Post-cutoff USGS observations remain verification only and are not used to fit or update the predictive distributions. The canonical GDPC climate-index covariate is sliced consistently to the cutoff-specific bundle; it is not treated as an operational forecast product or as a verification target.

As noted in HE-2, the five cutoffs were retained only when the forecast inputs, retrospective products, and forecast covariates were all archive-feasible and version-consistent. The evaluation folds were therefore determined by timing and data availability, not by forecast performance.

\end{responsebox}

Under the refreshed HE-2 publication authority, the revised comparison shows the same main conclusion more cleanly: \texttt{exAL-M-T1} has the lowest 28-day CRPS in all five rolling-origin cutoffs. \begin{commentblock}

Reviewer 1 Comments to the Author

This paper develops a Bayesian quantile regression approach, based on asymmetric Laplace distributions, to predict and forecast streamflow in the Santa Cruz region of the US. The work appears strong in the development of the Bayesian models and the novelty in the extension of inference methods is accepted. However, the improvement of forecasts through dynamical error correction is a common strategy and the novelty of this part may need to be elucidated further. The paper is framed as a forecasting paper, however the forecasting component is limited and robust forecast evaluation is absent. I recommend that some substantial revisions, mainly structural, are required before proceeding with publication. My comments below don’t cover everything, but are intended as a push into reshaping:
\end{commentblock}
\responseheading
\begin{responsebox}
We agree that, in its original form, the manuscript gave too much emphasis to model development and not enough to forecast validation. The revised manuscript addresses that directly by centering the forecasting analysis on multiple rolling-origin cutoffs over a 28-day horizon for GloFAS and the Bayesian predictive distributions, adding a separate eight-day NWS-horizon comparison, comparing the proposed method against simpler Bayesian alternatives and raw forecast products, reporting both CRPS and targeted quantile diagnostics, and using only the most recent forecast ensemble available at each cutoff.

We also revised how the contribution is stated. The revised manuscript presents the contribution as a Bayesian quantile-based correction-and-synthesis framework supported by rolling-origin forecast evaluation and selected-model interpretation, rather than treating dynamic discrepancy correction alone as the central novelty. The remaining issues concerning motivation, model-to-results linkage, and presentation are addressed point by point below.
\end{responsebox}

Forecast performance is assessed directly on those forecast windows using CRPS together with targeted quantile diagnostics. \commentheading{\RoneM{5}}

The revised analysis does this through five rolling-origin cutoff-based folds, which are the time-ordered analogue of cross-validation for the operational forecasting problem. In each fold, the forecast origin is fixed, only information available at that origin is used to fit and generate the predictive distribution, and post-cutoff USGS observations are used only for verification. The same design could be extended to broader tuning or validation over a larger set of feasible cutoffs, but in practice those cutoffs are constrained by archive/version consistency and should not be taken so densely that heavily overlapping forecast windows overrepresent the same hydrological regime. The revised manuscript now makes this evaluation setup explicit.
\end{responsebox}

Instead, precipitation enters as an external transfer covariate: zero-precipitation days are retained in the supplied covariate path, and precipitation intermittency enters through the transfer component and its deterministic engineered terms rather than through a separate precipitation model.
